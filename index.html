Overview

This workshop explores the fundamental question of how much capacity (in terms of parameters, architecture, and data) an AI model requires to effectively solve a specific task across a wide spectrum of application domains. This question is central to the ongoing community debate between large multi-task foundation models and smaller task-specific models. We will bring together researchers and practitioners from machine learning and various applications, including but not limited to NLP, geosciences, healthcare and biology to discuss theoretical foundations, empirical findings, and real-world applications.

As AI models grow in size and complexity, understanding the relationship between model capacity and task requirements becomes crucial for efficiency, sustainability, and practical deployment. Importantly, task objectives are not limited to predictive performance but can often include a number of potentially conflicting objectives such as robustness or efficiency. Accordingly, a key research question is which applications and task settings necessitate large foundation models, and under what conditions smaller models offer a more suitable alternative. Our workshop aims to significantly contribute to the ongoing debate by gathering research evidence from multiple application domains and discussing generic optimization approaches to investigate tradeoffs between predictive performance, efficiency and other metrics.

Topics of Interest

We invite submissions on (but not limited to) the following topics.

  • Theoretical and empirical bounds on model capacity.
  • TinyML: Efficient models for edge devices and low-power applications.
  • Multi-objective optimization: Balancing accuracy, efficiency, and robustness.
  • Domain-specific applications:
    • Natural Language Processing: Tasks in Industry 4.0, digital humanities, clinical settings, etc. involving highly specific, small text corpus.
    • Geosciences: Climate modelling, sensor networks.
    • Healthcare: Medical imaging, wearable diagnostics.
    • Robotics: Manipulation and grasping, human–robot interaction.
    • Biology: Protein folding, genomic analysis.

Submission Guidelines

Two formats, one venue.

Full Papers

Up to 16 pages (incl. references) presenting novel and original work. Must be fully anonymized. Authors can opt for publication in post-workshop Springer CCIS proceedings.

Abstracts

2–4 pages (incl. references) presenting already published work: conference papers, journal articles, demos, datasets, or public projects. Non-archival and non-anonymous.

All papers must be written in English and formatted in LaTeX, following the Springer LNCS format. All submissions will be reviewed by at least two Program Committee members via OpenReview.
